Effects of Loan and Borrower Features on Loan Status Outcome¶

Investigation Overview¶

In this investigation, I wanted to look at the Features of Loan and Borrower that could be used to predict the Loan Status Outcome (i.e. whether loan is performing or non-performing).

Dataset Overview¶

The original dataset contains 113,937 loans with 81 variables on each loan, including loan amount, borrower rate (or interest rate), current loan status, borrower income, and many others. The cleaned dataset contains 77,543 loans with 14 variables on each loan, including loan amount, borrower rate (or interest rate), current loan status, borrower income, and others. Missing datapoints were removed for completeness.

Distribution of Loan Status Outcome¶

Loan Status in the dataset take on two main categories: Performing Loans and Non-Performing Loans.

IMF's definition of Non-Performing loans are as follows:

  1. Payments of interest and principal are past due by 90 days or more
  2. At least 90 days of interest payments have been capitalized, refinanced or delayed by agreement
  3. Payments are less than 90 days overdue, but there are other good reasons to doubt that payments will be made in full".

For ease of analysis, the Non-Performing Loans includes loans that are Charged Off, Defaulted, and Past Due (> 1 day). The Performing Loans include loans that are Completed, Current, and FinalPaymentInProgress.

Features of Borrower¶

Features of Borrower can be classified into five main categories, each with one key variable:

  1. Earnings-related Features: Monthly Income
  2. Debt-related Features: Debt-To-Income Ratio
  3. Credit-related Features: Available Bankcard Credit
  4. Asset-related Features: Homeownership
  5. Aggregated Features: Prosper Score

Distribution of Earnings-related Feature¶

Monthly Income refers to the borrower's income per month. The hypothesis is that with higher income, the borrower has more financial resources. This leads to lower likelihood of Non-Performing Loans.

After removing outliers, monthly income is typically less than \$15,000.

Distribution of Asset-related Feature¶

Homeownership is a categorical variable referring to whether the borrower has a mortgage or a home under their name. The hypothesis is that with homeownership, the borrower can use the house as collateral, and will have lower Borrower APR, hence, lower likelihood of Non-Performing Loans.

Loan Status Outcome by Feature of Loan and Features of Borrower (Earning-related)¶

The Loan Status Outcome is affected by the Features of Loan and Borrower.

In the Monthly Loan Payment boxplot, Performing Loans has higher means and 25th/75th-percentiles than Non-Performing Loans. This suggests that higher Monthly Loan Payment corresponds to lower likelihood of non-performing loans. To investigate this hypothesis that higher Monthly Loan Payment corresponds to a higher income borrower, who have higher to lower likelihood of Non-Performing Loans. We can look at the correlation matrix between Monthly Loan Payment and Monthly Income. From the matrix, although the correlation is only moderately positive, it is also one of the strongest correlation in the matrix. This strengthens the hypothesis above. To confirm the hypothesis, we can do a logistic regression plot of the variables with Loan Status Outcome as response variable.

Loan Status Outcome by Features of Borrower (Asset-related)¶

The effect of homeownership on Loan Status Outcome is not obvious in the clustered bar chart, even though one can notice lower levels of homeownership for Non-Performing Loans.

Further investigation using the point plots show that homeownership have an effect on the loan's Monthly Payment, and Available Bankcard Credit. Since Monthly Payment and Available Bankcard Credit were shown above to have an positive effect on Loan Status, it strengthens the hypothesis that homeownership corresponds to lower likelihood of Non-Performing Loans
